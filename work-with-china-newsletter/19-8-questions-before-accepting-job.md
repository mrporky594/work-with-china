# 8 Questions You Must Ask Before Accepting a Job at a Chinese Multinational

**Author:** Wenchao Wang  
**Published:** June 23, 2026  
**Link:** [Read on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/8-questions-you-must-ask-before-accepting-job-chinese-wenchao-wang-ozuic)

---

I have quite a few foreign friends living in Singapore or China now, working for Chinese multinationals.

Many of them failed when first time landed in a role for large Chinese multinationals. They may quit the jobs during the first year.

I talked with several of them reflecting those reasons behind. Since we would expect large Chinese multinationals shall be easier for them to build career success comparing to SMEs.

The difference, many times, came down to questions they asked, or didn't ask before signing.

Here are the 8 questions that matter most.

---

## 1. Who is the actual decision-maker I'll work with daily?

Job descriptions at Chinese companies often list a formal reporting line. But formal and real are rarely the same.

Ask directly: "Who will I be working with most closely day-to-day?" and "Who makes the final call on the decisions in my scope?"

If the answer is vague, or if the actual decision-maker is someone in China HQ you've never met, that's a conversation you need to have before you accept.

---

## 2. What happened to the last person in this role?

This is the most revealing question you can ask.

If they left after 18 months, why? If the role is newly created, what was it replacing? If there have been three people in this job in three years, that pattern is not a coincidence.

Ask tactfully. Listen carefully. The answer, and the discomfort around it, tells you more than the job description ever will.

---

## 3. How does HQ handle disagreement from the overseas team?

This is the question most candidates never think to ask.

You need to know: when a local team pushes back on a HQ direction, what actually happens? Do they listen? Do they override? Do they simply stop responding?

Ask for a real example: "Can you share a time when the overseas team raised concerns about a HQ decision? How was that handled?"

If they can't give you one, or if the example they give ends with "HQ decided anyway," plan accordingly.

---

## 4. Is this a genuinely global role, or global in name only?

Many Chinese companies describe roles as "global" when they mean "we want someone who can represent us to Western partners."

The difference matters enormously for your day-to-day scope, your autonomy, and your career trajectory.

Ask: "What decisions am I empowered to make independently?" and "What requires HQ sign-off?"

The answer will tell you whether you're a strategic partner or a well-paid translator.

---

## 5. What does success look like in year one, and who decides?

Vague success criteria are a trap. You can work hard for a year and still be told it "wasn't quite what we needed" with no clear reason why.

Push for specifics: measurable outcomes, defined milestones, and who evaluates your performance and against what standard.

In Chinese companies, performance reviews are often relationship-dependent. If the person evaluating you barely knows you, that's a risk to price in.

---

## 6. What's the real reason this role is open right now?

Sometimes it's growth. Sometimes it's that they've tried twice and it hasn't worked. Sometimes it's a restructure that everyone except you knows about.

Ask the question directly. You won't always get the full truth — but the hesitation, the careful framing, and what they choose not to say will give you a lot of signal.

---

## 7. How does the company manage cultural friction on the team?

Every Chinese multinational has cross-cultural tension. The question is whether it's being managed or ignored.

Ask: "What's the biggest cultural challenge your overseas team has faced, and how did you work through it?"

Companies that have thought about this will have a real answer. Companies that haven't will pivot to talking about "our diverse culture."

---

## 8. What support will I have for the first 90 days?

Onboarding at Chinese companies is often sink-or-swim. You may be expected to be fully productive from week one with minimal context, no formal handover, and no one clearly assigned to help you navigate the informal environment.

Ask what deliberate support looks like. If the answer is "you'll figure it out, everyone does," you've just learned something important about how much this company invests in making foreign talent succeed.

---

## Final Thoughts

These questions aren't about being difficult. They're about having an honest conversation before a major commitment on both sides.

The Chinese companies worth joining will welcome this level of diligence. The ones who find it uncomfortable are giving you useful information too.
