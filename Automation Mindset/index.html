Chapter 8 — Mastering Your Automation Journey

The Five Towers of Automation · Maturity Model · Automation Discovery

The Five Towers of Automation

Processes usually have a primary focus on one of these top-level goals. These core objectives, in turn, roll up to departments — for example, HR teams focus on employee experience. We call these the towers of automation, and each tower is named according to both the area of the business and the associated outcome. When we set out to execute an automation strategy, we advise that leaders organize their approach around these five towers.

Tower of AutomationPrimary Organizational Objective
Customer experienceCustomer retention and expansion
Employee experienceEmployee retention and productivity
Supply chain efficiencySupplier relations & efficiency
Front officeRevenue
Back officeOperational excellence

The five towers cut across departments, teams, systems, and even top-level processes. In each tower, there are core, basic processes that nearly every company will recognize. The whole point of the new automation mindset is to fuel ideas and creativity, ultimately leading to competitive differentiation.

We need to promote creativity but with enough structure to direct our team's activities toward larger objectives. Creativity also needs a starting point. The best ideas are rarely all original. They are simply pushing the boundary of what has been done before — our ideas should "stand on the shoulders of giants."

Automation Discovery

Finding the right opportunities for automation in each company requires some effort. Given the uniqueness of each business, the options make certain approaches more attractive than others. In order to get started with your automation discovery, we recommend investing in discovery workshops for the tower of automation with both technical and nontechnical stakeholders. They will often focus on one or two core processes from end to end with an understanding of the process objectives.

Workshop steps:

  1. Agree on the objectives and metrics for the current process
  2. Outline how the process operates today
  3. Identify the points in the process where automation can positively impact the target outcomes
  4. Flag any compliance and approval steps required
  5. Estimate the impact of implementing all the corresponding timescost saved, risk reduced, improved experience

Many consulting firms and automation providers run discovery workshops much like this. An automation discovery workshop can help mindset in a structured way and help generate support from the large and more complex processes, assessing the business owners. While high-impact automation candidates are by no means the only options, they are obvious starting points.

The Power of Back-office Automation

Back-office automation is linked to other functions, such as front office or employee experience. Some key benefits that can be realized across the organizations are listed below.

BenefitOverview
Top-line growthAs automation eliminates the need for paper-based processes and reduces the number of manual touchpoints, employees can focus on more valuable tasks that can positively impact the top-line growth of the company. This can have a cascading effect of improving customer experiences and even act as a competitive differentiator for the business.
Reduced cycle timesAutomating end-to-end processes can reduce the cycle time through reduction in manual touchpoints and reduction in approval times. It can also improve adherence to service level agreements (SLAs) due to reduced variability in the process.
Improved data analyticsAs automation records all the steps during the process, the data can be better used to carry out process and data analytics, which can provide valuable insights of the process.
Reduced errorsWith reduced manual intervention along the process, chances of human errors are drastically reduced, so your clients can count on your systems to run efficiently.

Chapter 16 — The Automation Ecosystem

RPA · BPMS · iPaaS · API Management · ETL/ELT · Unmanaged Tools

Robotic Process Automation (RPA)

Origins: Software test automation, "green screen" scraping, and macros — recording actions via GUI and playing them back to automate manual tasks.

Age: 40+ years. Focus: Pure efficiency. Saving human work hours on manual tasks. "Better, faster, cheaper" is the mantra, with the goal of reducing headcount.

Aliases: Bots, process automation, robotic desktop automation (RDA), task automation.

RPA is a tool that mimics the actions of a human to automate interactions with applications through the screens normally reserved for people to use. An RPA bot replicates exactly these steps by simulating mouse and keyboard strokes to click buttons and fill in forms. Market growth for RPA is projected to expand from $1.7B in 2020 to $9B by 2025 according to IDC.

RPA is most successful when used in situations where a system has no alternative ways to read or update the data stored within it — for example, a supplier webpage that has no APIs.

Gaps and Limitations

  • Scale: It does not scale well. Companies struggle with broad automation initiatives based exclusively on RPA. It is a lot of work to maintain a large volume of bots due to their fragility and frequent support requirements.
  • Governance: It is hard to govern. RPA platforms struggle with org-wide deployments. The ability to have a broader set of people across the company building automations is rarely successful.
  • Maintenance: It can turn into tech and economic debt. The army of bots demands another army of bot managers. If operational overhead is not carefully considered, optimistic ROI calculations can quickly be eroded.
Although RPA often gets called low-code software, it doesn't always work out that way. It is typically not easy to learn, fast to deploy, or easy to maintain. RPA may be easier to use than resorting to custom code, but it falls short of the ideal democratization canvas.

Business Process Management Suites (BPMS)

Origins: BPMS tools existed as early as the 1980s in tools such as FileNet; the category was defined by Gartner in the early 2000s.

Age: 30+ years. Focus: Business processes-centric modeling and workflow execution — task management and forms for human workflow steps, process execution modeling, monitoring, and analytics.

Aliases: Business process management (BPM), intelligent business process management (iBPM), business process automation (BPA), business rules management system (BRMS).

BPM platforms were developed to help organizations better understand and improve their processes. BRMS are designed to manage complex business rules. BPA platforms were designed to automate business processes. Over time, the same capabilities were built into function-specific applications like CRM and ERP systems.

Gaps and Limitations

  • Speed: Outcomes are powerful — but slow. Success is measured in years. A huge percentage of the BPM consultant ecosystem has moved on to RPA for quicker wins, higher margins, and lower-hanging fruit.
  • Size: BPM tools are sometimes called "big iron" software, for the most mission-critical processes. While BPM can achieve powerful outcomes, it is being left behind as companies turn to other less rigid categories for faster outcomes. BPM is too focused on process without a firm grounding in an easy ability to execute tasks and system actions. RPA is too focused on tasks without a bigger picture view of the process. The answer is somewhere in between.

Integration Platform as a Service (iPaaS)

Origins: Middleware. The original player was Teknekron Information Bus, followed by Tibco.

Age: "Integration platform" is 30+ years old. "As a Service," 15 years old.

Aliases: Integration platform as a service (iPaaS), enterprise service bus (ESB), middleware, enterprise application integration (EAI).

Focus: Often referred to as "plumbing," integration technology is for moving data between platforms via APIs, files, or direct database connectivity.

iPaaS platforms integrate multiple apps, syncing datasets, initiating actions in other tools based on data events. They enable data to move from one system to another at varying schedules — batched integrations (hourly, nightly, weekly) and in near real-time where actions are processed within seconds as business events occur.

Modern iPaaS solutions are much different from the legacy ESB and middleware tools. They typically run in the cloud and are significantly less focused on complex message routing. They typically have:

  • Strong connectivity to many apps and services via APIs, events, files, or databases
  • Robust data transformation capabilities
  • The ability to manage and respond to business events and interact with event/message streaming services

Gaps and Limitations

  • Cloud claims: There is a big difference between cloud-native platforms and historically on-prem platforms that have simply been ported to the cloud. Only cloud-native platforms truly make use of auto-scaling, fault tolerance, and utility-like benefits.
  • Complexity: Traditional integration platforms are complex, technical tools requiring experts with computer science degrees. More modern platforms have adopted the low-code movement and therefore become strong candidates for democratization, plasticity, and orchestration. But buyer beware, not all iPaaS fit this mold.

API Management (APIM)

Origins: As APIs have grown more common and valuable, systems designed to publish and manage them have evolved.

Age: Formal APIM systems began circa 2009. Aliases: API gateway, API portal, API platform, API lifecycle.

Focus: APIM solutions focus on the management of company APIs and the creation of new endpoints — an organized, standardized, and controlled set of APIs for use across the company.

API management tools focus on two elements: API connectivity and API discoverability. The API gateway acts as a layer between the API caller and the downstream system, offering authentication, advanced security and encryption, rate limiting, caching, and other functions. APIM tools also enable companies to manage their vast set of APIs — imagine a company with 500 different applications, each with an average of 50 API endpoints (25,000 different API endpoints).

Gaps and Limitations

  • Pure-play limits: The focus of APIM is on connectivity and discoverability. The missing link is the logic that makes up the API. APIs provide the most value when they can be used as building blocks to encapsulate a set of actions across several other APIs — most APIM platforms do not do this.
  • API "religion": A frenzy of API-led architecture has led many companies down a false path. Building a vast library of APIs as reusable components sounds smart, but have we made any positive impact on our business? Leveraging APIs as building blocks is the right way to go. However, building APIs as the solution, rather than simply a component of the solution, results in a lack of focus on the ultimate outcome.

ETL (Extract, Transform, Load) and ELT (Extract, Load, Transform)

Origins: Originates from the very start of integration. Age: 30+ years.

Aliases: Data pipeline, data loader, data ingestion, extract transform load (ETL), extract load transform (ELT).

Focus: ETL/ELT load bulk data from applications and other data sources into a centralized repository such as a data lake or warehouse.

The primary difference between ETL and ELT is where the transformation happens. With ETL the tool extracts data from the source, transforms it to match the data model, and loads it into the data warehouse. ELT focuses on the extract and load, leaving the transformation to the data warehouse. ELT is the more modern approach providing increased speed to load data, ability to retransform historical data, and reduced complexity.

Gaps and Limitations

  • High volume, only: ETL and ELT tools work best for a very narrow use case of moving high volumes of data from edge applications to a data warehouse. When used as intended, they outperform other tools in the market.
  • Related use cases: These tools struggle with app-to-app integration, event-based automation, or process orchestration. ETL/ELT tools are the ultimate definition of specialist tools. They should be used for your reporting and analytics project but not for automation.

Chapter 17 — Enterprise Automation

Low-Code Unified Experience · Capability Map

With all our builders using a common platform, we break down silos and eliminate the fragmentation of knowledge that we often see with siloed delivery teams. Our cross-functional communication, visibility, and troubleshooting improves, and ultimately our organizations begin to operate as one unified force. Without a technology that unifies our teams, we can't achieve our objective of a cohesive approach to automation. Therefore, low-code / no-code (LC/NC) is a central requirement of any EAP.

This core of a unified experience must be supported by a broad range of capabilities to address the needs for process, data, and experience integration.

Chapter 18 — The New Operating Model

Centralized · Hybrid · Distributed · PowerCo Case Study

Automation Operating Models

Most companies don't stop to think about how they will deliver their automations. They often simply default to the approach they are using today. While "Steve in IT" is likely an amazing and talented individual, automation is not the responsibility of a single person in the company. It must be built into the very DNA of the company. This requires a well-thought-through operating model to define how teams across the organization will work together to transform the company.

ModelDescriptionOptimized For
CentralizedA central team takes requests for automations from the rest of the company. This team then prioritizes, builds, and operates the automations.Control
HybridA centralized delivery team augmented by a few additional teams who also deliver automation projects. The central team also takes on a new role: ensuring all teams are building in a consistent way, enabling them to be effective, and providing governance.Balance
DistributedFlips the typical central model around. It enables many teams to automate, and the central team focuses only on enablement and governance.Speed

Companies most often use these models in stages. Start centralized for control and governance, transition to hybrid as the organization becomes skilled, then move to distributed for speed. By starting slow, the company invests in controls and governance early, then later unlocks the speed of a distributed approach. Maintaining a strong central governance team is critical to a successful distributed delivery approach.

Case Study: PowerCo

Dan decided to take the gradual approach to work PowerCo up to a distributed operating model. He implemented an automation center of excellence (COE) and began evaluating integration and automation platforms. He knew from their past project failures that choosing a platform that could be used by all teams was going to be critical. After evaluating many tools, they found a platform that was low-code, easy to use, scalable, and had strong governance capabilities.

Phase 1 — Centralized: The team built the first automations for the HR program. In parallel they created strong governance processes and an enablement program to bring new teams onboard to automate.

Phase 2 — Hybrid: Once the program gained momentum, they began automating cross-functional processes such as payroll-finance reconciliations. They onboarded finance team members to help — multiple teams now building in the platform.

Phase 3 — Distributed: The team doubled down on enablement by building an automation resource center (a webpage for any new team interested in automation to learn how to get involved) and created a community for people to share ideas and success stories. This established a multi-team decentralized model with strong governance.

Role Descriptions

Technical Specialists

These technical experts should be elevated into a role that allows them to enable, guide, and support the broader community of builders. They should also be leveraged for the most difficult and most technically complex automation use cases. Technical specialists should also build shared services, guardrails, and other components to help other less-technical automation builders.

IT Administrators

These team members have a wealth of technical knowledge and with the right tools can streamline and automate a large number of IT operations. They can also be part of fusion teams to provide specialized technical expertise where needed.

Business Technologists

With such a strong business acumen and often detailed knowledge of the applications and data, these team members can both build automations and support business power users by providing the additional knowledge around concepts such as error handling, performance, and security.

BigOps Analysts

BigOps is a movement creating new roles, which are often the biggest drivers of automation in any company. These individuals understand the business processes inside and out. They also have deep expertise in the applications and their underlying data due to their frequent involvement with reporting. These roles can exist either in IT or in the line of business.

Business Power Users

Business power users represent team members who could work in any department in any role. These are individuals with a strong drive to make positive changes for the organization. They want to improve processes, and they want to help do it. For them to be good candidates to build automations, they do need a basic understanding of the business process and potential data or applications involved.

Business Stakeholders

This group can also work in any department and in any role. They are primarily identified by the fact that they don't have the desire to personally automate any processes. Our recommendation is to respect this wish. Forcing someone to participate in an automation program is not a recipe for success. Many of these people will eventually transition into business power users or even into BigOps analysts. These team members will often share ideas and requirements but will not build automations themselves.
