It's Not Culture. It's Trust. — Why I Started Work With China Newsletter

By Wenchao Wang · December 2025 · Read on LinkedIn

Over the past two decades, I've seen too many global executives and professionals around me go through the same quiet pattern:

They joined Chinese companies with genuine excitement — but left quietly within a year.

The reasons seem complex. And yet, not complex at all.

Most of them aren't failing. They're simply entering an environment whose rules, expectations, and decision logic were never made explicit — not in the interviews, not during onboarding, not even months into the role.

At the root of these breakdowns is one thing: a huge gap in cross-cultural trust.

And it's this gap that led me to create this Work With China newsletter.

The Missing Guide for Global Professionals Working with Chinese Companies

For 20 years, I've worked across China, Singapore, Australia, and beyond — visiting more than 2,000 companies in 15+ countries, working with multinational teams, and observing one pattern again and again:

Global professionals misunderstand how Chinese companies build trust.

Chinese companies misunderstand how Western talent makes decisions.

Both sides are smart. Both sides are capable. But both sides often fail not because of strategy — but because of misaligned expectations, communication styles, and different cultural logics.

The Work With China newsletter exists to bridge exactly that gap.

Who this newsletter is for

  • Evaluating a role or offer from a Chinese company
  • Interviewing with Chinese leadership
  • Already working inside a China–global structure
  • Navigating communication gaps, shifting expectations, or trust issues
  • Trying to understand how decisions actually get made in a Chinese company
  • Wanting fewer surprises and more clarity in their China–global career

What I will write about

  • How Chinese companies really operate — culture, trust, decision-making
  • How global talent should evaluate job offers from Chinese firms
  • How to avoid common misunderstandings between HQ and overseas teams
  • What foreign managers should know before working with Chinese leadership
  • Frameworks for trust alignment, communication, and cross-border leadership
  • Real stories from Chinese multinationals going global — successes & failures
  • Tools for building a sustainable China–global career path

Nothing theoretical. Everything based on real cases, real companies, and real conversations.

5 Things People Get Wrong About "Working with China"

By Wenchao Wang · April 14, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

A German mechanical engineer friend of mine has never set foot in China. Not once. Yet for the past two years, he's been the European market consultant for a drone company headquartered in Shenzhen, helping them navigate EU regulatory frameworks, build distributor relationships in Germany and the Netherlands, and translate product specs into language that German industrial buyers actually trust.

The Shenzhen team's European revenue tripled. He's never needed a visa.

Here's what most people get wrong: they treat "Work with China" as a geography problem. But the real question isn't where you are. It's what kind of relationship you've built.

1 | Most people think "Work with China" means going to China.

Today, over 500,000 foreign professionals interact daily with Chinese companies — and a significant portion of them have never lived in China. They're consultants in Singapore, lawyers in London, engineers in Stuttgart, marketing directors in São Paulo.

If you can deliver what's needed — market access, technical credibility, regulatory expertise — Chinese business partners will work with you remotely. Geography creates proximity. It doesn't create trust.

2 | "Work with China" is not one thing. It's three different relationships.

Work for Chinese companies — you're an employee or contractor inside a Chinese org structure. Requires understanding hierarchy, decision speed, and face.

Study in or learn from China — you're building knowledge. The longest runway but builds the deepest roots.

Partner with Chinese companies — you're an external collaborator. Trust logic is different: competence first, relationship second.

Mixing up which mode you're in is one of the most common mistakes.

3 | Remote collaboration with China isn't a pandemic hangover — it's becoming the default.

Chinese companies going global need local experts who stay local, because that's where their customers are. WeChat Work, Feishu (Lark), and internal video systems have matured. The work happens remotely. The relationship gets maintained through deliberate, high-quality touchpoints.

4 | What Chinese companies actually need isn't language or cultural performance.

Chinese companies move fast. Decisions that take 3 months in a European corporate can happen in 3 weeks in Shenzhen. But relationship-building moves slow.

The people who thrive hold both rhythms simultaneously. They can sprint on deliverables and be patient on relationships. They don't confuse urgency with intimacy.

5 | The people winning aren't the ones who know China best. They're the ones China trusts most.

Knowledge of China helps. But trust is what opens doors. Trust comes from three things: showing up consistently, delivering what you say, and making the other person look good in front of their own team.

When you help your Chinese partner succeed internally, they will protect you externally. That's the reciprocity logic.

5 Reasons Why Chinese Companies Are Trusted Less Than Their Products Deserve

By Wenchao Wang · April 17, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

In 2019, I sat across from a Chinese sales director in a hotel lobby in Kuala Lumpur. His company's product outperformed European competitors by two years in R&D. The price was 30% lower. Their Southeast Asia revenue was still 70% behind their European rivals.

"Because they don't know if they can trust us."

① Trust ≠ "I believe you won't cheat me"

In Western B2B culture, trust is built on compliance and transparency. Chinese companies have figured out compliance. But "transparency" isn't about legal transparency — it's about cultural legibility. Can a partner read your decision-making process? When I can't read the logic, I don't call it "fast." I call it unpredictable. And unpredictable = untrustworthy.

② Speed destroys trust signals

In China, saying "yes first, solve problems later" signals agility and commitment. In the West, a quick yes signals: "they don't understand what they're agreeing to." The same behavior — opposite interpretation.

③ Localization ≠ Translation

Real localization means your thinking process is legible to the local market. It means your team knows why a British client needs two weeks to make a decision that your Chinese HQ would make in two hours. Most Chinese companies haven't done this work. Their English website exists. Their mindset hasn't shipped.

④ The missing trust proxy

Every successful Chinese company in global markets — DJI, Midea, BYD, Haier — has a "bridge person." Not just a translator. Someone who understands the internal Chinese rhythm and carries genuine credibility in the local market. These people are rare. The gap between a local hire who doesn't understand the Chinese side and Chinese staff who struggle locally — that's where trust goes to die.

⑤ The data gap is real

Research from CEIBS found that Chinese companies' brand trust scores in overseas markets average 47 percentage points lower than comparable European and American brands. The product quality gap? Usually less than 10%. A 10% quality gap. A 47-point trust gap. This isn't a product problem. It's a trust infrastructure problem.

The companies that crack this aren't the ones with the best product or the biggest marketing budget. They're the ones who invested in being readable to local partners, local hires, and local customers.

4 Types of Global Professionals Who Can Work with China

By Wenchao Wang · April 21, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

① The Overseas Chinese Professional

The person everyone assumes has the advantage. They speak the language. They understand the culture. But both sides don't fully trust them. Chinese teams treat them as "not quite Chinese anymore." Western colleagues assume they're "automatically on the Chinese side."

The typical mistake: assuming that cultural fluency from childhood still maps onto today's business reality. China's corporate world in 2024 is nothing like the China they grew up in.

② The Internationally-minded Chinese Professional

Based in China, with foreign company experience or overseas education. The profile Chinese companies most actively recruit when going global.

The classic trap: they become a human translation machine rather than a decision-maker. They let themselves be used as a relay station instead of insisting on being the architect of the strategy.

③ The Foreigner Already in China

Being physically present is a real advantage — context, relationships, speed of response. But if their Mandarin doesn't clear a certain threshold, they remain an outsider with an insider's schedule.

Presence without language access is living in a foreign country inside an expat bubble.

④ The Foreigner Who Has Never Been to China

The most underestimated group. For Chinese companies going global, the outside perspective is exactly what's needed. A German logistics partner who knows European supply chain law cold? That's leverage.

The real mistake: paralysis. They spend six months doing research before making a single call. The entry barrier isn't knowledge. It's confidence in the value they already have.

The Rarest Profile of All

The person who can be trusted by a Chinese boss and read by a Western partner at the same time, in the same room. "Bilingual credibility" — not language, but credibility. This person is extraordinarily rare. Most people optimize for one side and sacrifice the other.

3 Chinese Business Behaviors That Confuse Westerners — And What They Actually Mean

By Wenchao Wang · April 23, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

Cultural differences aren't obstacles. They're data. They're signals from a system that has different inputs, different constraints, and different optimization targets. The question isn't "why do they act like this?" The question is: "What problem does this behavior solve in their system?"

01 | Speed — Not a Habit. A Survival Tool.

Chinese companies move fast because the cost of being wrong is lower — and the cost of being slow is higher. Market feedback loops are compressed. A product can go from beta to mass adoption in 90 days. Competitors can copy your feature in 60.

Launching imperfectly and iterating beats planning perfectly and launching late. Speed is not a personality trait. It's a survival strategy that evolved under specific market conditions.

02 | Guanxi — Not Nepotism. A Trust Infrastructure.

Guanxi is a trust substitute system — it emerged precisely because institutional trust (contracts, courts, enforcement mechanisms) was unreliable for most of China's modern commercial history.

When a Chinese counterpart introduces you to their network, they're extending their personal reputation as collateral. That's a significant act of trust — and it should be treated as one.

03 | Silence in Meetings — Not Emptiness. Calculated Caution.

Silence means: consensus has not yet formed, and speaking prematurely carries risk. The cost of expressing a public opinion in Chinese workplace culture is higher than in most western contexts.

People wait. They gather signals. They speak when they're confident the ground is stable. This isn't passivity. It's risk management applied to speech.

The fix isn't to push harder for opinions in the room. It's to create lower-stakes channels — bilateral conversations, written input, smaller groups — where the cost of speaking is lower.

Final Thought

Survivors memorize the behaviors. Experts decode the system. Which one do you want to be?

— Wenchao Wang

5 Things Westerners Get Wrong About How Chinese Managers "Trust"

By Wenchao Wang · April 30, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

2019, Shanghai. A European VP flew in for a "final signing meeting." Three dinners, two factory tours, six months of emails. He was sure the deal was done. The Chinese side asked about his family. Talked about everything except the contract. He flew home without a signature.

① Trust in China is not a prerequisite. It's a result.

In the West, you sign a contract, then build trust over time. In China, trust must exist before the contract means anything. The document is almost a formality.

② "Guanxi" is not networking. It's a trust ledger.

A running account of mutual obligation and reciprocal reliability. You deposit through favors, introductions, showing up. You withdraw when you need something. And you can't open the account the day you need the withdrawal.

③ Sharing a meal is due diligence, not small talk.

How you handle toasts, whether you reciprocate, how you treat the junior staff at the table — all of it is being read. The Chinese counterpart isn't making conversation. They're running a character assessment.

④ Silence after a meeting doesn't mean "no." It means they're watching.

They're checking if you're patient. If you're stable. If your enthusiasm was performative. Sometimes no response is the test.

⑤ Trust doesn't scale with titles. It scales with time.

Sending someone new — even someone senior — can signal the opposite of seriousness. Chinese business partners prefer to work with a consistent, familiar contact over time. Swapping faces resets the trust clock.

What this means: Don't assume a signed agreement means the relationship is secure. The relationship is the agreement. Invest early. Be present. Show consistency. Patience is not passivity — it's the strategy.

5 Things the GoPro Story Tells You About Working for a Chinese Company

By Wenchao Wang · May 25, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

GoPro is for sale. Not the cameras. The whole company. A 98.7% market cap decline. Meanwhile, Insta360's flagship camera costs $559. GoPro's costs $329. The Chinese brand charges more, holds bigger market share, and GoPro can't compete.

1. "Cost advantage" is the story people stopped updating

Insta360's revenue exceeded $900M in Q1–Q3 2025. GoPro's was less than a third of that. GoPro's gross margin collapsed from 32.1% to 4.3%. The real competitive edge isn't cost — it's the speed at which Chinese companies turn user feedback into product cycles.

2. Speed changes what kind of people they need to hire

When Xiaomi opened a European R&D center in Munich in 2025, they hired designers from Lamborghini, BMW's M4 GT3 program, and Mercedes-Benz. These aren't diversity hires. They're people brought in because the product needs to meet the expectations of the world's most demanding buyers.

3. The window is structural, not cyclical

In 2025, China's total outbound direct investment reached $174.4 billion — 11,048 overseas enterprises across 153 countries. Each one needs people who can run it. The real volume is in the tens of thousands of companies pulling in $200M a year, moving into new markets. Some are paying $500K for a single VP.

4. Most foreign professionals measure themselves against the wrong criteria

Chinese companies don't need one more person who is almost, but not quite, Chinese. They need the person you already are — the local relationships, the cultural instincts, the ability to walk into a room in Berlin or Dubai and be treated as an insider.

5. The people who moved early built something real

Someone who joined Huawei more than ten years ago — when everyone said it was too risky — used those relationships to found a PR and government relations firm for Chinese companies going global.

The question isn't whether Chinese companies are winning. The question is whether you're positioned to be part of how they do it.

4 Types of Chinese Companies — And Why Getting This Wrong Costs You Two Years

By Wenchao Wang · June 10, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

A friend works for a large state-owned enterprise in the Middle East. Good salary. Stable position. But he's done with everything by noon. "I feel like a bolt in a machine, just spinning in place." He walked into the wrong type of company.

① State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs)

Owned or majority-controlled by the government. Staggering scale, well-resourced and stable. But decisions travel through layers. Seniority matters more than immediate results. SOEs hire foreigners mainly for highly specialized technical roles or Belt and Road projects needing local-facing staff. Salary structures require multiple approvals — there isn't much flexibility.

② Private Companies

Large private companies (Huawei, ByteDance, BYD): Their global push creates direct demand for foreign talent. But they're expanding internationally far faster than they're building cross-cultural management capability. That gap is where you can make your mark.

Mid-sized private companies ($50M–$200M revenue): No name recognition, but they need you more, and they're more willing to give you real responsibility. Their overseas business is validated, but the local team to sustain it hasn't been built yet. That gap is your entry point.

Advice: When young, start at a large private company for the brand credential. Once you have experience, move to a mid-sized private for faster promotions and visible impact.

③ Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprises (WFOEs)

Apple, IKEA, Tesla in China. A practical stepping stone if you want China experience without jumping fully into a Chinese company. You're in a real Chinese market environment, but your management chain still feels familiar.

④ Joint Ventures

Jointly owned by a Chinese and foreign party. Power structures are often complicated. Before joining any JV, ask directly: "Who actually calls the shots here?"

How Do You Choose?

  • No track record yet → large private (brand credential)
  • Already have experience → mid-sized private (responsibility)
  • Want familiar structure → WFOE
  • Specific industry collaboration → JV (but figure out who runs things first)
  • Specialized technical + stability → SOE

I Evaluate Chinese Companies the Same Way I Pick Watermelons

By Wenchao Wang · June 15, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

A Singaporean friend asked: How do you know if a Chinese company is actually worth joining? He had three questions: Is this company solid, or just looks solid? Can I build real trust as an outsider? Where is my ceiling?

The Full Checklist

  1. What do the financials actually look like?
  2. Is the founder's direction consistent, or does it shift every six months?
  3. Does the title mean anything?
  4. Are the equity and options real?
  5. Did the top person meet you personally?
  6. What are the cultural signals that aren't in the contract?

The Watermelon Method

Hold the watermelon close, knock once, and listen. A deep, hollow thud — ripe. A high, tight sound — not ready. Reading a company works the same way. Sometimes one knock is enough. You knock in the right place, hear what comes back, and you already know.

Where Do You Knock?

The people at the top. Senior leadership, and the shareholders behind them.

If the CFO is the owner's wife, the COO is a cousin, procurement is all family — that's a signal. Their trust travels along bloodlines. As an outsider, there may be a door that stays closed.

But if you see professional executives who aren't relatives, old university friends of the founder, early investors who were classmates — that's a different echo. A trust circle that has expanded beyond family. A company that might have a seat for someone who earns their way in on merit.

Pop Mart: A Case Study

Founder Wang Ning, COO Si De, CFO Yang Jingbing — all from the same Peking University Guanghua MBA program. But the person running Pop Mart's entire international business is a Korean national named Moon Deok. He sits on the board as an executive director, inside the innermost circle.

A foreigner made it into the core circle of a Chinese company. That fact alone is the echo that comes back when you knock.

How Do You Get Into That Kind of Trust Circle?

Consider an EMBA in China. Joint programs between Chinese and foreign universities are still open. If your Mandarin is strong, go for a Chinese-language cohort. If not, choose programs with more entrepreneurs. Peking University, Zhejiang University — programs where more people have started their own thing.

8 Questions You Must Ask Before Accepting a Job at a Chinese Multinational

By Wenchao Wang · June 23, 2026 · Read on LinkedIn

I have quite a few foreign friends living in Singapore or China now, working for Chinese multinationals. Many of them failed when first time landed in a role for large Chinese multinationals. They may quit the jobs during the first year.

The difference, many times, came down to questions they asked, or didn't ask before signing. Here are the 8 questions that matter most.

① Who is the actual decision-maker I'll work with daily?

Job descriptions at Chinese companies often list a formal reporting line. But formal and real are rarely the same.

Ask directly: "Who will I be working with most closely day-to-day?" and "Who makes the final call on the decisions in my scope?"

If the answer is vague, or if the actual decision-maker is someone in China HQ you've never met, that's a conversation you need to have before you accept.

② What happened to the last person in this role?

This is the most revealing question you can ask.

If they left after 18 months, why? If the role is newly created, what was it replacing? If there have been three people in this job in three years, that pattern is not a coincidence.

Ask tactfully. Listen carefully. The answer, and the discomfort around it, tells you more than the job description ever will.

③ How does HQ handle disagreement from the overseas team?

This is the question most candidates never think to ask.

You need to know: when a local team pushes back on a HQ direction, what actually happens? Do they listen? Do they override? Do they simply stop responding?

Ask for a real example: "Can you share a time when the overseas team raised concerns about a HQ decision? How was that handled?"

If they can't give you one, or if the example they give ends with "HQ decided anyway," plan accordingly.

④ Is this a genuinely global role, or global in name only?

Many Chinese companies describe roles as "global" when they mean "we want someone who can represent us to Western partners."

The difference matters enormously for your day-to-day scope, your autonomy, and your career trajectory.

Ask: "What decisions am I empowered to make independently?" and "What requires HQ sign-off?"

The answer will tell you whether you're a strategic partner or a well-paid translator.

⑤ What does success look like in year one, and who decides?

Vague success criteria are a trap. You can work hard for a year and still be told it "wasn't quite what we needed" with no clear reason why.

Push for specifics: measurable outcomes, defined milestones, and who evaluates your performance and against what standard.

In Chinese companies, performance reviews are often relationship-dependent. If the person evaluating you barely knows you, that's a risk to price in.

⑥ What's the real reason this role is open right now?

Sometimes it's growth. Sometimes it's that they've tried twice and it hasn't worked. Sometimes it's a restructure that everyone except you knows about.

Ask the question directly. You won't always get the full truth — but the hesitation, the careful framing, and what they choose not to say will give you a lot of signal.

⑦ How does the company manage cultural friction on the team?

Every Chinese multinational has cross-cultural tension. The question is whether it's being managed or ignored.

Ask: "What's the biggest cultural challenge your overseas team has faced, and how did you work through it?"

Companies that have thought about this will have a real answer. Companies that haven't will pivot to talking about "our diverse culture."

⑧ What support will I have for the first 90 days?

Onboarding at Chinese companies is often sink-or-swim. You may be expected to be fully productive from week one with minimal context, no formal handover, and no one clearly assigned to help you navigate the informal environment.

Ask what deliberate support looks like. If the answer is "you'll figure it out, everyone does," you've just learned something important about how much this company invests in making foreign talent succeed.

These questions aren't about being difficult. They're about having an honest conversation before a major commitment on both sides.

The Chinese companies worth joining will welcome this level of diligence. The ones who find it uncomfortable are giving you useful information too.
